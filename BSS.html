Service Sector Q&A

Unit 1: Features of Service

Services are defined by five core characteristics that distinguish them from physical goods and shape how they are marketed and consumed.

  • Intangibility: Services are experiences that cannot be seen or touched before purchase, creating uncertainty. Marketers use tangible cues like a professional environment to signal quality.
  • Inseparability: Services are often produced and consumed simultaneously, making the service provider a critical part of the service itself.
  • Variability (Heterogeneity): The quality of a service can vary greatly depending on who provides it and when. Firms use standardization and training to manage this inconsistency.
  • Perishability: Services cannot be stored for later use. An unsold airline seat or empty hotel room represents lost revenue that can never be recovered.
  • Lack of Ownership: Customers purchase the right to a service or experience, not ownership of a physical object. Marketing focuses on the benefits and value created.

Unit 1: Classification/Types of Service

Services can be categorized using several frameworks to better inform a firm's marketing and operational strategy.

Based on the Nature of the Service Act

  • People Processing: Tangible actions on a person's body (e.g., healthcare, haircuts).
  • Possession Processing: Tangible actions on a physical possession (e.g., car repair, laundry).
  • Mental Stimulus Processing: Intangible actions on a person's mind (e.g., education, entertainment).
  • Information Processing: Intangible actions on a person's intangible assets (e.g., banking, legal services).

Based on Delivery Mode

  • People-Based: Service quality depends on the provider's skill (e.g., consultants, plumbers).
  • Equipment-Based: Service is delivered primarily by technology (e.g., ATMs, vending machines).

Based on Customer Involvement

  • High-Involvement: Requires active customer collaboration (e.g., personal training).
  • Low-Involvement: Requires minimal customer interaction (e.g., self-service kiosks).

Based on Business Orientation

  • Profit-Oriented: The main objective is financial gain (e.g., private hospitals, airlines).
  • Non-Profit-Oriented: The primary goal is social welfare (e.g., public libraries, charities).

Unit 1: Customers expectations in Services

Modern customers expect a sophisticated, holistic experience that goes beyond basic service. Businesses must meet these heightened expectations to remain competitive.

  • Connected and Consistent Journeys: Customers expect a seamless experience across all channels (online, in-store, mobile) and dislike repeating information.
  • Deep Personalization: Customers want to be treated as unique individuals and expect companies to use their data to provide tailored recommendations.
  • Speed, Efficiency, and Convenience: Fast response times, quick issue resolution, and multiple support options (omnichannel) are critical.
  • Trust, Transparency, and Data Protection: Customers are protective of their personal data and expect companies to be transparent about its use.
  • Empathy and Being Heard: Customers need to feel that their concerns are understood and valued. An empathetic approach can turn a negative situation into a positive one.
  • Proactive Service: Leading companies anticipate potential issues and offer solutions before the customer is even aware of a problem.

Unit 1: Factors influencing consumer expectations

Consumer expectations are shaped by a mix of personal history, company communications, and external influences.

  • Past Experience: A customer's previous interactions with the same brand or other best-in-class companies set the standard.
  • Marketing and Communication: A company's explicit promises (advertising) and implicit cues (high pricing suggesting high quality) directly shape expectations.
  • Word-of-Mouth and Social Proof: Recommendations from friends, family, online reviews, and social media are highly influential.
  • Personal Needs and Situational Factors: A customer's unique needs and temporary circumstances can intensify expectations (e.g., a person in a hurry expects speed).
  • Perceived Service Alternatives: If a customer is aware of many high-quality alternatives, their expectations for any single provider will be higher.

  • Product: The core service offering, defined by the intangible benefits it provides.
  • Price: The amount a customer pays, which also serves as a key indicator of service quality.
  • Place: The location and channels through which the service is delivered and made accessible.
  • Promotion: Communication strategies used to inform and persuade customers, often focusing on benefits and testimonials.
  • People: All human actors involved in the service delivery‚Äîemployees and customers‚Äîwhose behavior is critical to the experience.
  • Process: The procedures, mechanisms, and flow of activities by which the service is delivered.
  • Physical Evidence: The tangible cues and environment where the service is delivered, which customers use to evaluate quality (e.g., facility design, website).

Unit 1: features of marketing research

Marketing research is the systematic process of gathering and analyzing data to support strategic marketing decisions by reducing uncertainty.

  • Systematic and Continuous Process: It follows a structured sequence of steps and is an ongoing activity.
  • Objective and Unbiased: Research must be conducted without personal bias to ensure findings are credible.
  • Wide and Comprehensive Scope: It covers all aspects of the marketing mix, including product development, consumer behavior, and competitive analysis.
  • Data-Driven and Analytical: It uses both quantitative and qualitative methods to transform raw data into actionable insights.
  • An Aid to Decision-Making: Its ultimate purpose is to provide relevant information to help managers make better decisions.
  • Balances Value and Cost: Involves a careful cost-benefit analysis to ensure the value of the information justifies the expense.

Unit 1: steps in new Service development cycle

New service development is a structured, human-centered process focused on designing both the customer experience and the internal processes that support it, typically following four iterative phases.

  • Phase 1: Align: This strategic phase focuses on defining the project's purpose and ensuring all stakeholders share a common understanding of the goals.
  • Phase 2: Research: This phase involves conducting deep, empathetic research to understand the needs and behaviors of both customers and employees using methods like interviews and surveys.
  • Phase 3: Ideate: Based on the research, this creative phase involves brainstorming a wide range of potential solutions through collaborative workshops.
  • Phase 4: Prototype: The most promising ideas are turned into low-fidelity, testable versions (e.g., storyboards, role-playing) to learn quickly from real users before full-scale development.
  • Implementation and Iteration: After a successful prototype, the service is launched. The cycle is iterative, meaning feedback is continuously gathered to refine and improve the service over time.

Unit 2: different between organized and unorganized retailing

The Indian retail sector is a mix of organized and unorganized formats, distinguished by their structure, scale, legal status, and operational methods. Organized retail refers to modern, large-scale chains like supermarkets, while unorganized retail includes traditional, small, independent businesses like local kirana shops.

Feature Organized Retailing Unorganized Retailing
Definition Modern, large-scale formats (supermarkets, hypermarkets) with systematic management. Traditional, small, independently owned businesses (kirana stores, street vendors).
Legal Status Registered with the government; complies with tax and labor laws. Not registered; operates outside formal government regulations.
Scale & Investment Large-scale operations with high capital investment and multiple outlets. Small-scale, single-location businesses with low capital investment.
Employment Formal employment with fixed salaries, benefits, and job security. Informal employment with daily wages, no fixed hours, and no benefits.
Customer Experience Standardized, enhanced experience with modern amenities (A/C, parking). Familiar and convenient for local communities with personalized relationships.
Product Assortment Wide variety of branded products. Limited variety, primarily unbranded or local products.

Unit 2: Trends in retailing

The Indian retail sector is undergoing a rapid transformation driven by technology, evolving consumer behavior, and real estate development.

  • Robust Physical Expansion: Physical retail is growing, with a significant year-on-year increase in retail space absorption in top cities.
  • Dominance of Key Categories: Fashion & Apparel and Food & Beverage continue to lead retail leasing, with Jewellery emerging as a strong third category.
  • Shift Towards Experiential Retail: Consumers are seeking immersive experiences, leading to a boom in Family Entertainment Centers.
  • Integration of Online and Offline (Omnichannel): Digitally native brands are opening physical stores to enhance customer engagement.
  • Emergence of New Commerce Models: Quick Commerce (delivery in under 30 minutes) and Trend-First Commerce are rapidly growing segments.
  • Demographic and Geographic Shifts: Gen Z is a major e-retail shopper group, and over 60% of e-commerce transactions now originate from Tier-II and III cities.

Unit 2: major causes responsible for the growth of organized retailing in india

The expansion of organized retail in India is driven by a combination of powerful socio-economic, demographic, and policy factors.

  • Rising Consumerism and Changing Lifestyles: Urbanization and global exposure have created more aspirational consumers who seek the convenience of organized retail.
  • Increased Disposable Income: Economic growth has expanded the middle class, giving more people the purchasing power for branded goods.
  • Increase in Working Women: Working women often prefer the one-stop convenience of modern retail formats like supermarkets.
  • Favorable Demographics: India's large, young population is more receptive to modern retail concepts.
  • Entry of Corporate and Foreign Players: Major corporations have brought massive investment, technology, and global best practices to the sector.
  • Infrastructure and Real Estate Development: The construction of highways and modern shopping malls has provided space for large-format stores.
  • Economic Reforms and Liberalization: The opening of the economy allowed Foreign Direct Investment (FDI), fostering a competitive environment.

Retailing is broadly divided into store and non-store formats based on whether transactions occur at a physical location. Store retailing involves brick-and-mortar shops, offering a sensory experience. Non-store retailing uses remote channels like e-commerce, offering convenience and broader reach.

Unit 2: Types of Store formats

Retail stores are classified based on their product assortment, size, pricing, and service level.

  • Department Stores: Large stores offering a wide variety of product categories in different departments (e.g., clothing, home goods).
  • Supermarkets: Large, self-service stores primarily selling groceries and household items on a high-volume, low-margin model.
  • Convenience Stores: Small stores in high-traffic locations offering a limited selection of everyday items at higher prices.
  • Specialty Stores: Focus on a narrow product category but offer a deep assortment within it (e.g., electronics stores, bookstores).
  • Discount Stores: Offer a wide range of products at prices lower than traditional retailers.
  • Category Killers: Large specialty stores that dominate a product category with deep selection and aggressive pricing.
  • Warehouse Stores / Clubs: Large, no-frills stores selling merchandise in bulk at discounted prices, often requiring a membership fee.
  • Chain Stores: A series of retail outlets owned by the same company, benefiting from centralized management.

Unit 2: Guideline of effective store planning

Effective store planning involves the strategic design of the physical space to enhance the customer experience and maximize sales.

  • Understand Customer Behavior: Use tools like heat mapping to analyze customer traffic flow and optimize product placement.
  • Optimize Store Layout: Design an intuitive layout that guides customers logically through the merchandise.
  • Create an Inviting Ambience: Use proper lighting, ensure cleanliness, and reflect the brand's identity.
  • Implement Effective Visual Merchandising: Use clear signage and create compelling displays that tell product stories.
  • Incorporate Technology: Integrate technology like QR codes for product information or self-checkout kiosks.
  • Provide Amenities and Focus on Service: Offer amenities like seating and ensure high-quality service from well-trained staff.
  • Continuously Analyze and Adapt: Regularly gather customer feedback and analyze sales data to adapt the store environment.

Unit 2: Challenges of retailing in india

The Indian retail sector faces several deep-seated structural and administrative challenges that hinder its growth.

  • Inadequate Power Supply: The single most significant obstacle, causing frequent outages and loss of annual sales.
  • Poor Access to Finance: A major hurdle, forcing most retailers to rely on retained earnings for investment.
  • Corruption: A pervasive issue, with government inspections often resulting in requests for bribes.
  • Land-Related Problems: Challenges in securing suitable land, dealing with costly registration, and obtaining permits.
  • High Tax Rates: The tax burden is a major concern, particularly for larger, organized players.
  • Low Level of Competition: Competitive pressure is low by international standards, reducing the incentive for firms to innovate.

Unit 3: Scope of LPO

Legal Process Outsourcing (LPO) is a specialized sector where legal work is outsourced to providers in lower-cost countries like India, which has become a global hub.

  • Market Size and Growth: The Indian LPO market is substantial and growing rapidly, with projections for significant expansion.
  • Scope of Services: LPOs in India typically handle work related to foreign laws, including contract drafting, legal research, e-discovery, and compliance assistance.
  • Career Opportunities: LPO offers an alternative career path for law graduates with a corporate environment and better work-life balance.
  • Limitations and Challenges: The work is often perceived as "commodified," which can limit professional development in core legal skills. This can lead to lawyers losing touch with domestic law.

Unit 3: Features of ATM

An Automated Teller Machine (ATM) is a self-service banking device that allows customers to perform financial transactions without a human teller, offering 24/7 convenience.

  • Core Functions: ATMs perform essential banking operations like cash withdrawal, deposits, balance inquiries, and funds transfers.
  • Hardware Components: Key parts include a card reader, keypad, display screen, cash dispenser, deposit slot, receipt printer, CPU, and a secure vault.
  • User-Centric and Security Features: ATMs offer 24/7 accessibility and use a Personal Identification Number (PIN) for security. Many have multilingual interfaces and some are incorporating biometrics.

Types of ATMs

  • On-Premise ATMs: Located at bank branches, these are typically more advanced, multi-functional machines.
  • Off-Premise (White-Label) ATMs: Standalone machines in convenient locations, often owned by independent companies and offering limited functions for a fee.

Unit 3: Features of logistics

Logistics management is the part of supply chain management that plans and controls the efficient movement and storage of goods, services, and information from origin to consumption.

  • Transportation Management: Involves selecting the best transport mode, planning efficient routes, and tracking shipments.
  • Inventory Management: Focuses on maintaining optimal stock levels to meet customer demand without high holding costs.
  • Warehousing and Materials Handling: Manages the physical storage of goods, including receiving, storing, picking, and packing.
  • Order Processing and Fulfillment: Covers the entire cycle from receiving a customer's order to its successful delivery.
  • Information and Control Systems: Relies on technology like TMS and WMS for real-time visibility and data-driven decision-making.
  • Packaging: Protects the product during transit and facilitates efficient handling and storage.
  • Demand Forecasting: Uses data and trends to anticipate customer needs, allowing for better inventory and transportation strategies.

Unit 4: Features of E-Commerce

E-commerce refers to the buying and selling of goods and services over the internet. Its core features distinguish it from traditional commerce.

  • 24/7 Accessibility: Online stores are always open, allowing consumers to shop at any time, from anywhere.
  • Global Reach: E-commerce transcends geographical boundaries, enabling businesses to reach a worldwide customer base.
  • Process Automation: Key processes like payment processing and inventory updates are automated, improving efficiency.
  • Digital Payment Systems: E-commerce relies on secure electronic payment methods like credit cards and digital wallets.
  • Data-Driven Personalization: Platforms collect and analyze customer data to offer personalized product recommendations.
  • Reliance on Digital Marketing: Businesses use digital marketing strategies like SEO and social media to attract customers.

Unit 4: Reason for success of E-Commerce in india

The success of e-commerce in India is due to its strategic adaptation to the unique characteristics of the Indian market.

  • Solving for Access and Aspiration: E-commerce provided millions in smaller towns with their first-ever access to a wide variety of brands.
  • Adaptation to Local Payment Preferences (Cash on Delivery): COD was crucial in the early stages to build trust among consumers wary of online payments.
  • Leveraging Cultural Affinity for Festivals and Discounts: E-commerce giants created massive online shopping festivals around major Indian festivals.
  • The Mobile-First Approach: Companies developed user-friendly mobile apps, recognizing that most Indians accessed the internet on a smartphone.
  • Strong Investor Confidence: Massive funding allowed leading companies to invest in technology, logistics, and aggressive customer acquisition.
  • Improved Logistics and Last-Mile Delivery: Substantial investments in supply chain have enabled reliable delivery across the country.

The scope of e-commerce in India is vast and rapidly expanding, marked by a massive market size, deepening penetration, and continuous diversification.
